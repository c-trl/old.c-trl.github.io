zillow rent index

using python to compare and analyze zri data

Working in any urban area has its perks. Commuting is not one of them.

For the un-established young professional looking to make it in a big city, commuting can be one of the tougher parts of urban living. With this in mind, a trade-off needs to be made between time and money spent on traveling versus resources spent on not traveling. Does a short commute justify expensive living conditions? Does a comfortably affordable living condition justify a long commute?

Everyone has their own acceptable trade-offs and balances, however I became interested in visualizing one side of that balance in rent prices. In particular, I was interested in levels in New Jersey.

To do this, I used Python to work with Zillow's ZRI data. I was excited to use the Zillow Rent Index (ZRI) data because of Zillow's smoothing methodology to calculate their 'Zestimates' (wasn't as excited about their terminology). Zillow applies a 3-month moving average along with other adjustments to normalize their data. Working with this data seemed like a lot more fun than pulling numbers off of a government database.

After sorting through New Jersey data, I was not surprised to find that Hoboken is, by a long shot, New Jersey's most expensive city to rent in (going by $ per square foot). Favorable commuting distance to New York City, proximity to 2 New Jersey state highways and 2 Interstate highways, and some of New Jersey's better night life - it's no surprise Hoboken has the highest $ per square foot levels in New Jersey.

A comparison of most of the state's county seats gives a state-wide picture of the varying degrees (or lack thereof) in median rent levels throughout the state.

Consistencies throughout the state speak to how suburbanized much of the state is. On the other hand, comparing rent levels in suburban areas to levels in cities like Atlantic City, Newark, and Camden illustrate the dynamic levels of income inequality throughout the state.

The illustration of how diverse the state is led me to consider how rent prices may have affected (or been affected by) changes in the average unemployment rate state-wide. For those who can afford renting a new place, it only makes sense that a rise in unemployment may discourage new renters. Adversely, drops in unemployment may encourse new renters. The first trend I looked for in the ZRI data was a noticeable rise in median rent levels from late 2011 when the unemployment rate in New Jersey began to significantly drop.
